Not every AI idea deserves immediate investment. Some opportunities are high value and practical. Others are interesting but premature.

The challenge for leaders is separating the two.

## Start with an opportunity inventory

Before prioritizing, create a list of candidate opportunities. These may come from executive reflection, manager input, staff feedback, workflow review, or data analysis.

For each opportunity, clarify:

- What problem does it solve?
- Which workflow or department is affected?
- What evidence supports it?
- What value could it create?
- What readiness gaps or risks exist?

## Score against three lenses

A practical prioritization model should consider:

**Strategic Fit**  
Does the opportunity support current business priorities?

**Economic Impact**  
Could it reduce cost, improve capacity, increase speed, improve quality, reduce risk, or support revenue?

**Feasibility**  
Can the organization realistically execute it with current systems, data, people, budget, and risk tolerance?

## The goal is sequence

Prioritization is not only about choosing what to do. It is also about choosing what to delay, what to validate, and what foundation must be built first.

That sequence is what turns AI interest into an actionable roadmap.
