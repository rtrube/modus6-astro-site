Shadow AI is informal AI usage that happens outside a clear organizational policy, approval process, or governance structure.

It is not always malicious. In many cases, employees are simply trying to work faster, write better, summarize information, or solve recurring problems.

## The risk is lack of clarity

Without guidance, employees may not know:

- Which tools are approved.
- What data can be entered.
- Where confidential information should not be used.
- When human review is required.
- Who owns AI-related decisions.

This creates risk, but it also reveals demand. Employees are signaling that they need better ways to work.

## Governance should not block adoption

Good governance is not about stopping AI usage. It is about creating boundaries that support responsible adoption.

A practical policy should define acceptable use, data handling expectations, approval paths, review requirements, and areas where AI should not be used without oversight.

## Leadership owns the roadmap

If employees are already experimenting with AI, leadership has a decision to make. Ignore it and let usage spread informally, or create a responsible adoption path tied to business priorities.

The better answer is usually to make AI use visible, governed, and connected to a broader readiness roadmap.
